A Corridor in Bloom

By B Corridor Team · May 10, 2024

Spring has arrived along the B Corridor, bringing colour, life, and a welcome reminder of why this landscape matters. After a long, grey winter, the verges, hedgerows and riverbanks are waking up, and so are the creatures that depend on them.

Walk any stretch of the corridor this month and you will notice the change underfoot. What looked like bare ground in February is now a patchwork of green, threaded with the first flowers of the year. Each one is a small act of repair: roots holding soil, leaves feeding insects, blooms offering nectar to anything that passes.

What's flowering

The earliest colour comes from the humble pioneers of the hedge bank, plants we too often mow away before they have a chance to do their work. This spring, look out for:

  • Lesser celandine, low glossy stars of yellow that open with the morning sun.
  • Primrose and cowslip, soft and pale, favourites of early bees.
  • Dandelion, unfairly maligned and one of the single most important early nectar sources we have.
  • Blackthorn, frothing white along the older hedgerows before its leaves appear.

What's buzzing

Where there are flowers, there is movement. The first queen bumblebees are out, fresh from hibernation and searching for both food and a place to nest. Hoverflies drift between the celandines, and on warmer afternoons you may catch the season's first butterflies, brimstone and small tortoiseshell, tracing the line of the hedge.

A corridor is only as strong as the stepping stones that make it. Every flowering verge is one more place for life to rest, feed and move on.

This is exactly what the corridor is built to do: connect these small havens so that wildlife can travel safely between them. A pollinator patch outside a school, a no-mow meadow in a churchyard, a restored riverbank, on their own they are islands. Joined together, they become a route.

How you can help

You don't need a large garden, or any garden at all, to be part of this. The simplest thing most of us can do is also one of the most powerful: leave the mower in the shed a little longer. Letting grass and wildflowers grow through spring gives insects the food and shelter they need just when they need it most.

If you would like to do more, we would love to hear from you. Whether it is planting a few native flowers, joining a community planting day, or helping look after a patch near you, there is a place for everyone along the corridor.
